These Step-by-Step instructions guide you through the Sketch Map Tool and provide useful recommendations.MQC

Step-by-Step instructions

1
Create paper maps to collect data
1.1 Define your Area of Interest

Select your area of interest either by zooming into the map preview or with the search function in the sidebar.

Choose from different paper formats and a suitable page orientation. The dashed red line represents the boundaries of your area of interest, which will be printed on the Sketch Map.

Recommendations:
  • Adapt the extent of your selected area to your use case. The following questions should be kept in mind:
    • What should be mapped by the participants?
    • What should be visible on the map and in what level of detail?
    • Which areas should the map cover?
  • Adapt the area extent to the paper size you want to use.
  • The area is too big? Create multiple Sketch Maps.
  • Aspects you might want to consider for the selection of the area of interest:
    • Ensure orientation on the map: streets (and their names) and some points of interest (POIs) should be visible.
    • Cover all relevstrongant areas for your use case: e.g. if studying flooding, cover especially those areas near rivers, creeks and sinkholes as well as critical infrastructure which might be affected by the flood event.
1.2 Map Quality Check

The generated Sketch Map uses a base map layer of OpenStreetMap (OSM). The Map Quality Check helps to evaluate the fitness of the area of interest for the purpose of participatory mapping based on a quality analysis of the OpenStreetMap data with the HeiGIT ohsome quality analyst - OQT. The fitness report can be downloaded as PDF file by clicking on the blue button. It includes an evaluation of the suitability of the local OSM data and recommendations for the subsequent field data collection.

Recommendations:
  • Before you use these OSM based maps of your area of interest for participatory mapping, please have a look at the analysis of the Map Quality Check to ensure that participants can orientate properly on the paper maps.
  • The analyses are focused on the Point of Interest (POI) density, the last-edit of amenities and streets, the used sources, as well as the saturation of amenity and the road network mapping. You can learn more about the analysis and each indicator in the generated PDF report.
  • The report includes useful recommendations to ensure that the generated maps are suitable for participatory mapping.

  • In case the OSM data of your area of interest is not sufficient for the purpose of participatory mapping, you can consult the Missing Maps project (ADD HYPERLINK) to improve the OSM data before using the Sketch Map Tool.
1.3 Export Sketch Map as PDF

The generated Sketch Map for your selected area of interest can be downloaded as PDF file by clicking on the blue button.

THEN:  Data collection in the field

At the moment the Sketch Map Tool detects red, blue and black markings. The tool does not differentiate between blue and black marking and combines these to one output result. The detection works best if the marked areas are filled out entirely. The tool cannot detect overlapping markings.

Recommendations:
  • Use thick felt-tip pens with intense colours.
  • If you have many participants or you want participants to provide a lot of different information, it is recommended to use individual Sketch Maps for each purpose/topic or per participant.
  • Prepare the same set of pens for all participants/groups for better comparison of the results.
  • If you collect additional information from the participants with other data collection methods such as Kobo Collect or ODK, add a number on the Sketch Map and save this number in the data collection questionnaire.
2
Paper to Geodata: Automatically digitize collected data
2.1 Scan or photograph your marked Sketch Maps
{# TODO: check max file size#}

After the field data collection, scan or photograph the marked Sketch Maps. The colour detection works best when scanning the Sketch Maps. When taking a picture instead, make sure that the QR-Code is complete and that all globes are visible on the image. The upload works for Jpg or png files and the files may not be larger than 50 MB.

Recommendations:
  • If you cannot scan, the photos must be taken from above in a 180-degree angle (parallel above the sketch map) to produce the best possible result.
  • Please upload a complete image of the sketch map and do not try to cut out the map from the photo.
  • Take care of the lighting conditions when taking a picture.
2.2 Upload your Sketch Maps

Scan or photograph your marked Sketch Maps after data collection. Then, upload one or more marked Sketch Maps at once.

  • It is also possible to upload more than one sketch map at the same time.
  • If you are experiencing problems during the upload process, check out the FAQ section below.
2.3 Download results as Geodata

Download the sketches in different geodata formats (Geotiffs, Vector data or All-In-One-QGIS projects).

  • You can download the georeferenced Sketch Map as a GeoTIFF or the colour-detected shapefiles (for both red and blue/black markings) in one ZIP-folder.
FINALLY: Import Geodata in QGIS
  • You can open the colour-detected shapefiles and the further analyses directly in a QGIS project.
  • When you open the QGIS project it should look similar to this example and show all the markings on your sketch maps. If anything looks strange, please check your uploaded sketch maps and if necessary, scan them again or take new pictures.
  • Opened as a new QGIS project the automatic colour-detected markings on the sketch map can be further analysed. One analysis is the count of overlapping areas between the markings. The analysis should work automatically within the sketch map tool but if problems occur the analysis is a combination of the union and count-duplicates scripts → check Screenshots of Analysis tools (combined)
  • To finally see the analysed result of the count duplicates you need to adapt the symbology to your needs and the number of analysed sketch maps!

FAQ

What are OSM data?
OpenStreetMap data are freely accessible geodata. You can find out more about it here.
What are the quality levels for OSM data used in the tool?
The OSM data are classified with a traffic light system. Green stands for good suitability of the data, yellow for possible problems when carrying out a sketch map study with OSM based sketch maps, and red for probable problems. Recommendations are also given on what to consider based on the results.
Which analyses are performed and why?
You can read more about the analyses included in the Sketch Map Tool in this paper.
Why is it important to analyse the suitability of OSM data before creating a sketch map?
OSM data is used for the creation of the sketch map. If the OSM data in a study area are lacking some important features, they are missing on the maps as well. Thus the quality of the OSM data is directly related to the quality of the sketch maps. The features might be relevant for participants to orientate on the map.
For which research questions can the Sketch Map Tool be used?
The Sketch Map Tool has been primarily developed for research questions related to floodings. But as the quality indicators used like the density of landmarks, currentness of the data, data completeness and important sources are also important in other use cases, you can also use it to investigate a completely different research question, as long as you check that it fits your purpose.
How does mapping work with the Sketch Map Tool?
The local population can for example draw flooded areas such as streets, parks, etc. on the printed sketch map. The larger paper formats also allow to hold group discussions about flooded areas. The maps with the markings can be photographed and uploaded to the tool. The georeferenced TIFF and shape files can then be further analysed or used.
How large can my study area be?
The study area needs to be smaller than 50km². If you need quality analyses for larger areas, you can take a look at the OSM History explorer.

Where can I find the results of my analysis?
When you start an analysis, a link to the status page for your request will be shown. You can click on this link and either stay on this page or bookmark it and return later. As soon as your results are ready, a download link will be shown on this status page. If you click on it, you will see your results on a webpage, which also includes a PDF download button that allows you to download the results as a PDF.
Why are there a code and globes on the sketch maps?
These are required for geo-referencing. This is why it is important to have them on the sketch maps. If the code cannot be detected by the tool, i.e. you see an error message stating this; in this case you can also use the info next to 'code contents' on the sketch map. This information can be entered manually under 'Problems with the code detection?' on the upload page.

Can I access my results at a later point of time?
Yes. If you bookmark the status page, you can go back to it later and still find a working download link. But keep in mind that in future the results will likely be regularly deleted, which is why you should always store your results locally as soon as they are ready.

Can I see data that has been added to OSM just recently on the sketch maps and in the analyses' results?
For the sketch map generation the current OSM data are used and the new features should appear on the maps, for the analyses the ohsome API by the HeiGIT is used, which is updated regularly but might not contain very recently added data.
What do I have to pay attention to in the field?
Please make sure you do not accidentally mark the globes at the edge (they are needed to improve the automatic map detection). But if someone accidentally marked the globes, it is still possible to upload the sketch maps. Try the automatic detection of the map boundaries first, and if this does not work, you can use the workaround explained under 'Do the generated TIFF files look distorted?' on the upload page. Use thicker felt tip pens with intense colours. Currently, for the shapefile generation only red, blue and black are supported. Prepare the same set of pens for all interviewers. Add a number on the sketch map and save this number in the questionnaire as well, if you use one. You can find an overview of the important points here.
How does the geo-referencing work?
You can find your answer in the paper about the Sketch Map Tool in the paragraph about 'Image Processing'.
What do I have to consider when photographing the sketch maps to get the best possible results?
The images need to be in .jpg or .png format. The file may not be larger than 50MB. The geo-referencing works best if you scan the marked sketch map with a resolution of 300 dpi. The photos must be taken from above in a 180-degree angle (parallel above the sketch map) to produce the best result. If the generated GeoTIFF files look distorted, you can use the workaround explained under 'Do the generated TIFF files look distorted?' on the upload page. An overview of important points can be found here.
How do I check if the geo-referencing is correct?
You can open the TIFF file in a geographic information system (GIS), such as ArcGIS or QGIS, to check this. If you load the file there and insert a basemap (for example the OSM basemap) you can see if the geo-referencing has worked. The sketch map should match the base map. If this is not the case, you can find possible causes here.
Why does my generated TIFF file look strange?
Please first try to open the file in a geographic information system (GIS) such as ArcGIS or QGIS. The georeferencing may have worked even if the TIFF file does not look right. If it is still incorrect in the GIS, the map could not be properly detected by the tool on the photo for which there are various possible reasons. For example, the photo may have been taken from the wrong angle or the globes may have been painted over. You can find a detailed list of possible sources of error here. You can still upload your photo and digitize the markings without problems though. Just follow the workaround explained under 'Do the generated TIFF files look distorted?' on the upload page.
